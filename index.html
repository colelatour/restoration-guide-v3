Context & Background

The Great Apostasy

Understanding why followers of Christ believed divine authority was lost from the earth — and why it needed to be restored.

Historical Fact + Official Church Teaching

What is an Apostasy?

The word "apostasy" comes from the Greek apostasia, meaning a falling away or defection. In Christianity, it refers to the belief that after the death of Jesus Christ and His original apostles, essential doctrines, ordinances, and priesthood authority were lost or corrupted from the earth.

This is not merely a Latter-day Saint claim. Many Protestant reformers — including Martin Luther, John Calvin, and John Wesley — acknowledged that the early church had undergone significant corruption over the centuries.

Historical Fact

The First Council of Nicaea (325 AD) revised foundational Christian doctrine under Emperor Constantine — an event many historians point to as evidence of significant doctrinal change from the primitive church.

"Moroni Shows the Gold Plates to Joseph, Oliver, and David" · William L. Maughan · Church of Jesus Christ Media Library

What Does the New Testament Say?

"For I know this, that after my departing shall grievous wolves enter in among you, not sparing the flock. Also of your own selves shall men arise, speaking perverse things, to draw away disciples after them."

— Acts 20:29–30 (KJV), Paul's warning to the Ephesian elders

"Let no man deceive you by any means: for that day shall not come, except there come a falling away first..."

— 2 Thessalonians 2:3 (KJV)
Official Church Teaching

Reformation vs. Restoration

Protestant reformers sought to reform an existing church. Joseph Smith claimed to restore something entirely lost — including the authority, ordinances, and organizational structure of the original church established by Jesus Christ. Without priesthood authority, no amount of reform could reconstitute the saving ordinances.

Scholarly Interpretation

Historian Richard Bushman writes that Joseph Smith's claim was not that Christianity had merely drifted but that "the heavens had closed" and divine communication had ceased — making reformation insufficient. (Rough Stone Rolling, 2005)

A Fair Question

If God exists and established a church, why would He allow it to be lost? The Latter-day Saint answer draws on biblical precedent: the Old Testament records multiple apostasies among God's covenant people, always followed by prophetic restoration. The pattern of apostasy and restoration is, in this view, a recurring feature of religious history.

Spring 1820

The First Vision

The foundational claim of the Restoration — a young man's prayer and what he reported followed.

Historical Fact + Official Church Teaching

The Setting

In the early 1800s, western New York was swept by intense religious revivals known as the "burned-over district." Joseph Smith, then 14, found himself amid competing religious claims. Turning to James 1:5 — "If any of you lack wisdom, let him ask of God" — he decided to pray in a grove of trees near his family's farm.

The "First Vision" · Walter Rane · Church of Jesus Christ Media Library

Historical Fact

The "burned-over district" of western New York is well-documented. Whitney Cross's The Burned-Over District (Cornell University Press, 1950) remains the definitive secular history of this religious environment.

The Account

Joseph Smith recorded multiple accounts of the First Vision (1832, 1835, 1838, 1842). The most widely published is the 1838 account, canonized as Joseph Smith—History:

"I saw a pillar of light exactly over my head, above the brightness of the sun... When the light rested upon me I saw two Personages, whose brightness and glory defy all description, standing above me in the air. One of them spake unto me, calling me by name and said, pointing to the other — This is My Beloved Son. Hear Him!"

— Joseph Smith—History 1:16–17
Scholarly Interpretation

Multiple Accounts: A Problem or Evidence?

The accounts do differ in some emphasis — the 1832 account focuses on forgiveness of sins; the 1838 account adds detail about two distinct beings. The Church published a Gospel Topics Essay, "First Vision Accounts," which addresses these differences openly. Historian Steven C. Harper argues in Joseph Smith's First Vision (2012) that the differences are complements rather than contradictions — each account highlighting aspects relevant to its audience.

Official Church Teaching

Latter-day Saints teach that God the Father and Jesus Christ are separate, distinct beings — a doctrine foundational to the First Vision and a major departure from traditional Trinitarian Christianity.

1823–1830

The Book of Mormon

Another testament of Jesus Christ — its origin, translation, and significance to the Restoration.

Official Church Teaching

What Is the Book of Mormon?

The Book of Mormon is a volume of scripture used alongside the Bible by Latter-day Saints. It claims to be an ancient record of peoples in the Americas from approximately 600 BC to 421 AD, centered on the ministry of Jesus Christ — including His appearance in the Americas after His resurrection.

Joseph Smith said he translated the record from gold plates he retrieved from a hill in upstate New York (Hill Cumorah) under the direction of an angel named Moroni. The original 1830 edition was printed by E.B. Grandin in Palmyra, New York in a print run of 5,000 copies.

"Brother Joseph" portrait · Church of Jesus Christ Media Library

The Translation Process

Scholarly Interpretation

The exact method of translation is a subject of ongoing discussion. The Church's Gospel Topics Essay on Book of Mormon Translation acknowledges that Joseph used a "seer stone" placed in a hat for much of the translation — a detail now openly addressed by the Church.

Historical Fact

Eleven witnesses signed formal statements testifying they either saw the gold plates (Three Witnesses) or handled them (Eight Witnesses). None recanted their testimonies, even those who later left the Church under difficult circumstances.

The Witnesses

"We declare with words of soberness, that an angel of God came down from heaven, and he brought and laid before our eyes, that we beheld and saw the plates, and the engravings thereon..."

— Testimony of the Three Witnesses, Book of Mormon (Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer, Martin Harris)

"Elijah Appearing in the Kirtland Temple" · Dan Lewis · Church of Jesus Christ Media Library

Scholarly Interpretation

Internal Evidence

Scholars have noted features of the Book of Mormon consistent with ancient literary patterns — including chiasmus, a Hebrew poetic structure essentially unknown in 19th-century American literary culture. John Welch's 1969 discovery of chiasmus in the Book of Mormon sparked decades of analysis. The Neal A. Maxwell Institute at BYU has published extensive scholarship on the book's ancient linguistic features.

An Honest Note

Archaeological evidence for the civilizations described remains a subject of significant scholarly debate. The Church does not claim archaeology has "proven" the Book of Mormon. Questions remain; readers are encouraged to engage primary sources directly.

1829–1830

Restoration of the Priesthood

The authority to act in God's name — claimed to have been lost and then restored through divine messengers.

Official Church Teaching

What Is the Priesthood?

In Latter-day Saint theology, the priesthood is the authority and power of God delegated to man to act in saving ordinances. Without this authority, baptism and other ordinances are not considered valid — making reformation of existing churches insufficient. A new conferral from heaven was required.

The Aaronic Priesthood

Historical Fact

On May 15, 1829, Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery reported that John the Baptist appeared and conferred the Aaronic Priesthood, granting authority to baptize.

"Upon you my fellow servants, in the name of Messiah, I confer the Priesthood of Aaron, which holds the keys of the ministering of angels, and of the gospel of repentance, and of baptism by immersion for the remission of sins..."

— Doctrine and Covenants 13:1

The Melchizedek Priesthood

Shortly thereafter, Peter, James, and John appeared and conferred the higher Melchizedek Priesthood — including the keys of the kingdom of God — on Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery. This authority became the basis for Church governance and all higher ordinances.

Historical Fact

The Church was formally organized on April 6, 1830, in Fayette, New York, with six original members — the minimum legally required under New York state law. (D&C 20; Church History Topics)

Hill Cumorah, Manchester, New York — site of Joseph Smith's annual visits from the Angel Moroni · Wikimedia Commons · CC BY-SA 3.0

A Note on Evidence

The evidence for these events is primarily testimonial — the accounts of Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery, written contemporaneously. There are no independent historical witnesses to the visitations themselves. Readers are encouraged to study primary sources at josephsmithpapers.org.
